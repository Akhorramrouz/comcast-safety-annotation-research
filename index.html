Annotation Guidelines

Prompt Safety Study — Please read carefully before you begin annotating.

⚠ Important: This is an independent human annotation study. You must complete all annotations on your own, without consulting other annotators, and without using any AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or any similar system) at any point during the annotation process. Violations compromise the integrity of the dataset and may result in your annotations being excluded.

This study is part of a research project investigating how AI language models respond to prompts across different domains. Your role is to read each prompt and answer three structured questions about it. Your judgements will be used to build a high-quality ground-truth dataset for evaluating AI safety policies.

Your Role & Responsibilities

You are a human annotator. Your task is to read each prompt carefully and make independent, considered judgements. You are not evaluating the AI's response — you are evaluating the prompt itself.

What you will do

  • Read each prompt displayed on screen.
  • Select the domain category or categories the prompt belongs to.
  • Decide whether the prompt violates any of the safety policies shown for that domain.
  • Rate your confidence in that decision.
  • Submit your answers. The tool will automatically save your progress.

What you will not do

DON’T Use any AI assistant or chatbot to help evaluate a prompt.
DON’T Discuss specific prompts or your answers with other annotators before submitting.
DON’T Rush. Quality matters far more than speed.
DON’T Leave the page mid-session with unsaved answers — the tool will warn you if you try.

Annotation Steps

Step 1 — Category
Q1 — Select all categories that apply to this prompt.
Choose one or more of the predefined domain categories that best describe the subject matter of the prompt. You may select as many as apply, or none if the prompt does not clearly fit any category.

DO Select multiple categories if the prompt genuinely spans more than one domain.
DO Focus on what the prompt is asking for, not incidental words in the text.
DON’T Select a category simply because a keyword from that domain appears in the prompt.
Step 2 — Policy Violation
Q2 — Does this prompt's request violate any of the safety policies presented above?
On this screen, the safety policies for the prompt's actual domain are displayed. Read them carefully. Then decide: does what the prompt is asking for fall within the scope of any listed violation?

Yes, it violates a policy — the prompt is requesting something that one or more of the listed policies explicitly prohibit or flag as harmful.
No, it does not violate any policy — the prompt is a legitimate request that does not trigger any of the listed policies.

Tips for Q2

DO Read every policy rule before deciding — a violation may be in a rule you almost skipped.
DO Base your decision solely on the policies shown — not on your general moral intuition.
DON’T Assume a prompt is safe just because it uses polite or academic language.
DON’T Assume a prompt is harmful just because the topic sounds sensitive — context matters.
Step 2 — Confidence
Q3 — How confident are you in your response to the preceding question?
Confident — the prompt clearly does or does not match a policy; you have no meaningful doubt.
Uncertain — you have a considered opinion but acknowledge genuine ambiguity in how the prompt or policy could be interpreted.

Tips for Q3

DO Be honest. Marking uncertainty is valuable data — it signals edge cases for the research team.
DON’T Always select “Confident” to appear decisive. Overconfidence degrades data quality.

Common Edge Cases

The prompt asks about a sensitive topic but for legitimate reasons

A researcher asking how SQL injection works for a security audit is not the same as someone requesting code to attack a live system. Focus on the intent implied by the phrasing and whether the policy language covers it.

The prompt is ambiguous

If a prompt could be read either as benign or as a policy violation depending on context, annotate based on the most reasonable interpretation of a typical user sending that exact text. Mark Q3 as “Uncertain.”

The prompt spans multiple domains

Select all relevant categories in Q1. For Q2, the tool will show policies for the prompt’s actual ground-truth domain from the dataset — evaluate against those policies regardless of which categories you selected.

You are not a subject-matter expert in this domain

Do your best using the policy descriptions provided. If the technical detail of a prompt is beyond your expertise, rely on the plain-language description of the policy rule and mark Q3 accordingly.

Data Integrity & Ethical Conduct

  • Independent work only. Each annotation must reflect your own, unaided judgement. Coordination with other annotators before submitting skews inter-annotator agreement metrics.
  • No AI assistance. Using an AI tool to evaluate a prompt — even to “double-check” — invalidates your annotation. The entire purpose of this study is to capture human judgement.
  • No reverse-engineering the ground truth. Do not attempt to infer the “correct” answer from metadata, column names, or other visible information. Annotate based on what you read.
  • Consistency. Apply the same standard throughout. If you decide that a particular type of phrasing does not constitute a violation, apply that decision consistently across similar prompts.
  • Reporting concerns. If you encounter a prompt that you find distressing or that you believe should not be in the dataset, note the prompt number and contact the research lead rather than skipping it silently.

Practical Tips

  • Your progress is saved automatically after each submission — you can close the browser and resume later using your annotator code.
  • Each prompt is reviewed by exactly two annotators. Your contribution directly shapes the reliability of the final dataset.
  • There is no time pressure. A careful annotation session of 30–60 minutes is preferable to a rushed one.
  • If you accidentally close the tab before submitting Step 2, the browser will warn you. If you lose a partially completed annotation, simply re-open the tool — it will resume from the last saved prompt.
